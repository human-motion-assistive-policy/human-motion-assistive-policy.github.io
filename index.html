Learning Robust Policies for Whole-Body
Assistive Tasks via Dynamic Human Motion Generation

In submission

Abstract

Learning robot policies for physical caregiving assistance is challenging because assistive tasks require close-contact, whole-body coordination with humans who move dynamically, maintain balance, and exert interaction forces. Real-world data collection for such interactions is expensive and safety-critical, while existing simulation-based approaches often rely on quasi-static or minimally active human models that do not capture the dynamic nature of transfer assistance. In this work, we present a simulation-driven framework for learning robust whole-body assistive robot policies via dynamic human motion generation. We first train a physics-based human policy with an adversarial motion prior to generate biomechanically plausible human motions during physical human-robot interaction. The generated human acts as an active simulated care recipient, allowing the robot to experience physically coupled interaction rather than assistance around a static human model. We then collect robot demonstrations in simulation with a human-adaptive scripted controller that conditions its assistance behavior on the current human state. Finally, we train a 3D diffusion policy from the curated demonstrations using segmented human point clouds and robot joint positions as deployable observations. Experiments on Sit-to-Stand and Lift-and-Transfer tasks show that the learned policy achieves strong simulation performance in close-contact assistive scenarios. We also demonstrate preliminary sim-to-real transfer on a physical assistive robot for Sit-to-Stand assistance after lightweight fine-tuning with a small amount of real-world data. These results suggest that dynamic human motion generation provides a scalable path toward learning robot policies for whole-body assistive tasks.

Method Overview

Our training pipeline has three stages. First, we train a physics-based human policy with an Adversarial Motion Prior (AMP) and latent conditioning to generate dynamic physics-based human motions across varied human physical capabilities, body scales, poses, motion styles, and environments. Next, we simulate robot assistance with a human-adaptive scripted controller and curate successful interactions as robot demonstrations. Finally, we train a deployable assistive robot policy that maps segmented human point clouds and robot joint positions to robot delta joint actions.

Qualitative Comparison: Training Robot Policy with Mixed vs. Single Human Capability

Human capability means the human torque limit in simulation. Here we compare robot policies trained with different human capability distributions: one uses mixed capabilities, with both 1.0x and 0.25x humans, while the others use a single human capability.

In each paired rollout, the human and robot placement, running human policy, human scale, and human capability are the same. The only difference is the robot policy.

The rollouts below show that training with mixed human capabilities improves robustness: compared with policies trained only with 1.0x- or 0.25x-capability humans, mixed-capability training better adapts to the evaluated interactions and produces successful assistance.

Real-World Results


With only six minutes of real-world demonstration data for fine-tuning, the simulation-trained policy performs preliminary Sit-to-Stand assistance on a physical bimanual mobile robot, using only onboard RGB-D sensing and proprioception.
