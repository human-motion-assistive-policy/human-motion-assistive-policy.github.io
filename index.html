Learning Robust Policies for Whole-Body Assistive Tasks via Diverse Physics-Based Human Motion Generation

In submission

Caption for the first teaser video.

Abstract

Learning robot policies for physical caregiving assistance is challenging because assistive tasks require close-contact, whole-body coordination with actively moving humans. Real-world data collection for such interactions is expensive and safety-critical, while existing simulation-based approaches often rely on quasi-static or minimally active human models that do not capture the diversity of real human behaviors. In this work, we present a simulation-driven framework for learning robust assistive robot policies through diverse physics-based human motion generation. We first train a latent-conditioned physics-based human policy with an adversarial motion prior to generate biomechanically plausible and diverse human motions during human-robot interaction. We then use these human motions to collect robot demonstrations with a human-adaptive scripted controller, producing interaction data that covers diverse human poses, motion styles, body scales, and relative human-robot configurations. Finally, we train a 3D diffusion policy from the curated demonstrations using segmented human point clouds and robot proprioception as deployable observations. Experiments on Sit-to-Stand and Lift-and-Transfer tasks show that our learned policy achieves strong simulation performance. Ablations further show that increasing human motion diversity improves downstream robot policy performance. We also demonstrate preliminary sim-to-real transfer on a physical assistive robot for Sit-to-Stand assistance after lightweight fine-tuning with a small amount of real-world data. These results suggest that diverse physics-based human motion generation is an effective path toward scalable learning of whole-body assistive robot policies.
